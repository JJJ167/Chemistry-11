Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry is used to figure out how much of each chemical is needed or produced in a chemical reaction.

Example: A burger

This burger consists of:

  • 2 buns
  • 3 tomato slices
  • 2 cheese slices
  • 1 lettuce leaf
  • 2 patties

Let's turn this into a "chemical equation": 2 buns + 3 tomato + 2 cheese + 1 lettuce + 2 patties → 1 burger


Some of the ratios:

2 buns: 3 tomatoes

2 buns 3 tomatoes
2 tomatoes: 1 burger

2 tomatoes 1 burger
2 patties: 1 lettuce

2 patties 1 lettuce

etc.



  1. How many burgers can you make with 21 tomato slices?

    # of burgers = 21 tomatoes x 1 burger 3 tomatoes = 7 burgers


  2. How many cheese slices do you need to make 143 burgers?

    slices of cheese = 143 burgers x 2 cheese 1 burger = 286 cheese slices



In this example, the ingredients act as the "chemicals" (reactant) and the burger acts as the product.

Coefficients in a Chemical Reaction Equation

The coefficients in a chemical equation tell us the number of moles or molecules


Example: N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3

The coefficients in this equation tell us that there is one mole of nitrogen reacting with 3 moles of hydrogen to produce 2 moles of ammonia (NH3).

The mole ratios:

1 mol N2: 3 mol H2

1 mol N2 3 mol H2
1 mol N2: 2 mol NH3

1 mol N2 2 mol NH3
3 mol H2: 2 mol NH3

3 mol H2 2 mol NH3

Note: These mole ratios can be flipped, depending on what you are trying to solve for.



  1. How many moles of hydrogen gas (H2) are needed to produce 8 moles of ammonia (NH3)?

    # = 8 mol NH3 x 3 mol H2 2 mol NH3 = 12 mol H2


  2. How many moles of ammonia (NH3) are produced from 13.2 moles of nitrogen gas (N2), assuming excess hydrogen?

    # = 13.2 mol N2 x 2 mol NH3 1 mol N2 = 26.4 mol NH3

Now that we know how to convert between moles, we can bring back the mole unit to convert to other units!

RECALL:

Example:

  1. Reaction: Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)

    What volume (in litres) of H2 is produced at STP from 12.0 mol of Zn?

    2. L = 12.0 mol Zn x 1 mol H2 1 mol Zn x 22.7 L H2 1 mol H2 = 272.4 L = 272 L H2



  2. Reaction: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)

    What mass (in grams) of O2 is needed to produce 1295 mL of CO2 gas at STP?

    3. g = 1295 mL CO2 x 10-3 L 1 mL x 1 mol CO2 22.7 L CO2 x 2 mol O2 1 mol CO2 x 32.0 g O2 1 mol O2 = 3.65 g O2



Enthalpy

Heat can also be calculated using conversion factors. Think of heat as just another reactant (if endothermic) or product (if exothermic) in the chemical reaction.

Example: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l) + 587 kJ


Possible conversion factors with heat:

587 kJ: 2 mol H2

587 kJ 2 mol H2
587 kJ: 1 mol O2

587 kJ 1 mol O2
587 kJ: 2 mol H2O

587 kJ 2 mol H2O

Note: These conversion factors can be flipped, depending on what you are trying to solve for.


a) How many moles of H2 are needed to react to produce 293 kJ of heat?

mol = 293 kJ x 2 mol H2 587 kJ = 0.998 mol H2



b) How much heat is being released when 12.0 g of H2 reacts with excess oxygen?

heat = 12.0 g H2 x 1 mol 2.02 g x 587 kJ 2 mol H2 = 1,743 kJ = 1.74 x 103 kJ



Limiting and Excess Reagents

  1. Limiting reagent (limiting reactant): The reactant that gets completely used up in a chemical reaction.

  2. Excess reagent (excess reactant): The reactant that does not get completely used up in a chemical reaction.

Let's bring back the burger:

Recall:

This burger consists of:

  • 2 buns
  • 3 tomato slices
  • 2 cheese slices
  • 1 lettuce leaf
  • 2 patties

2 buns + 3 tomato + 2 cheese + 1 lettuce + 2 patties → 1 burger



  1. How many burgers can you make with 21 tomato slices?

    # of burgers = 21 tomatoes x 1 burger 3 tomatoes = 7 burgers

    However, what if there are only 10 burger patties available? Would you still be able to make 7 burgers?
    (assuming unlimited burger buns, cheese, and lettuce)

    No, because 10 burger patties can only make:

    # of burgers = 10 patties x 1 burger 2 patties = 5 burgers

    Therefore, burger patties are the limiting reagent. They get used up and limit the # of burgers that can be made.

    The rest of the ingredients are the excess reagents. Not all of it gets used up.

Note: the limiting reagent is not always the substance that exists in the smallest quantity.


Example:

2Al(s) + 6HCl(aq) → 3H2(g) + 2AlCl3(aq)

If 8.80 g of Al reacts with 70.0 mL of 1.50 M HCl:

a) Which is the limiting reagent?

Convert Al and HCl to moles of H2:

mol of H2 = 8.80 g Al x 1 mol 27.0 g x 3 mol H2 2 mol Al = 0.489 mol H2


mol of H2 = 70.0 mL HCl x 10-3 L 1 mL x 1.50 mol 1 L x 3 mol H2 6 mol HCl = 0.0525 mol H2

HCl produces less hydrogen gas (0.0525 mol) than Al (0.489 mol) → Therefore, HCl is the limiting reagent.


b) How many moles of H2 are produced?

In part a), we found that HCl is the limiting reagent. It can produce 0.0525 mol of H2 → Therefore, 0.0525 mol H2 is produced


c) How many grams of excess reactant are left?

Since HCl is the limiting, Al is in excess.

Convert the limiting reagent (HCl) to Al in grams. This determines how much Al has reacted.

grams of Al = 70.0 mL HCl x 10-3 L 1 mL x 1.50 mol 1 L x 2 mol Al 6 mol HCl x 27.0 g 1 mol Al = 0.945 g Al

Subtract the reacted amount from the initial amount.

8.80 g - 0.0945 g = 7.86 g Al is left over.


Percent yield

Actual Yield: The amount of product you got from your experiment.

Theoretical Yield: The amount of product you get if the experiment went perfectly (based on your calculations).

Percent Yield: Tells us how much product we actually got compared to how much we should have gotten if everything in the experiment went perfectly.


Think of it like this:

  • You plant 10 apple seeds.
  • You only ended up with 7 apple trees.

Theoretical Yield: You would've ended up with 10 apple trees if everything had gone perfectly.

Percent Yield: You only ended up with 7 apple trees because not everything went perfectly. Maybe some seeds had gone bad.


Percent Yield = Actual yield Theoretical yield

Example:

11.3 g of H2 reacts with excess N2. 56.0 g of NH3 had been collected. What is the percent yield?


Step 1: Find theoretical yield.

g = 11.3 g H2 x 1 mol H2 2.0 g H2 x 2 mol NH3 3 mol H2 x 17.0 g NH3 1 mol NH3 = 64.0 g NH3



Now we know:
  • Actual yield: 56.0 g
  • Theoretical yield: 64.0 g

Step 2: Find percent yield.

Percent Yield = Actual yield Theoretical yield

→

Percent Yield = 56.0 g 64.0 g = 0.875 x 100 % = 87.5 %

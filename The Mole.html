Avogadro's Number, Avogadro's Law and Atomic Masses

Avogadro's number: 6.02 x 1023

This number:

  • represents 1 mole.
  • is used to count atoms, molecules, compounds, particles, ions, etc.
  • is rounded (like pi).
  • must be considered for sigfigs.

Think of it like this:

1 pair = 2 items
1 dozen = 12 items
1 mole (mol) = 6.02 x 1023 particles






Avogadro's Law: If the temperature and pressure stay the same, the more gas particles there are (moles), the more space it takes up (volume). We use this law at STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure).

At STP, 1 mole of any gas will take up 22.7L of space.


This is the molar volume of a gas at STP.






Atomic mass: The mass of a single atom. The units are amu (atomic mass units).

  • Usually used for single elements
  • Can get it directly off the periodic table.
  • No conversion factors or calculations needed (unless you're trying to find the average atomic mass of an element with isotopes).

    • Example:

    • Atomic mass of carbon = 12.0 amu

Molar mass: The mass of 1 mole of a substance. The units are g/mol (grams per mole).

  • Based on atomic mass.
  • Used to convert between grams and moles.
  • Usually needs conversion factors when converting between different units.

    • Example:

    • Molar mass of carbon = 12.0 g/mol

    • Molar mass of H2O
      • H = 1.0 g/mol x 2
      • O = 16.0 g/mol x 1

      2.0 g/mol + 16.0 g/mol = 18.0 g/mol

    • Molar mass of CuSO4· 5H2O
      • CuSO4 = 159.6 g/mol
      • 5H2O = 90.0 g/mol

      159.6 g/mol + 90.0 g/mol = 249.6 g/mol










    Note: keep one decimal place.

Formula mass: The mass of a compound. The units are amu (atomic mass units).

  • Sum of the atomic mass of all the atoms in a compound.
  • Simple calculation but no conversion factors.

    • Example:

    • Formula mass of KNO3
      • K = 39.1 amu x 1
      • N = 14.0 amu x 1
      • O = 16.0 amu x 3

      39.1 amu + 14.0 + amu + 48.0 amu = 101.1 amu

Note: The values of atomic mass and molar mass are
the same, however they have different units.

Mole Conversions

We can use Avogadro's number, Avogadro's law, and molar mass connects units such as # of atom or molecule, mass (g), and volume (L).

We use these as conversion factors.

Examples:

*Remember to count sigfigs!*

  1. How many moles are there in 53.7g of H2O?

    2. Molar mass of H2O = 18.0 g/mol

    mass = 53.7 g x 1 mol 18.0 g = 2.98 mol



  2. What is volume at STP occupied by 15.2 mol of HCl(g)?

    volume = 15.2 mol x 22.7 L 1 mol = 345 L



  3. What is mass of 3.20 L of NH3(g) at STP?

    mass = 3.20 L x 1 mol 22.7 L x 17.0g 1 mol = 2.40 g



  4. How many atoms are there in 39.0 mL of N2O(g) at STP?

    # of atoms = 39.0 mL x 10-3 L 1 mL x 1 mol 22.7 L x 6.02 x1023molec 1 mol x 3 atoms 1 molec = 3.10 x 1021 atoms


Density Calculations:

d = m v


We use density to calculate the volume of solids, liquids, and sometimes even gases since 22.7 L/mol only applies to gases at STP.

If trying to find the density, we can use mass and volume or molar mass and molar volume at STP to find the density.

Examples:

*Remember to count sigfigs!*

  1. If there is 3.50 L of an unknown liquid that has a density of 1225 g/L, what is the mass of this liquid?

    2. mass = 3.50 L x 1225 g 1 L = 4.29 x 103 g



  2. What is the density of CO2 gas at STP?

    3. Density = Mass Volume = Molar mass Molar volume = 44.0 g/mol 22.7 L/mol = 1.94 g/L
